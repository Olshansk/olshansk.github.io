In the past, every online content publisher and mediator focused on maximizing reach and engagement.

Later, the conversation started shifting towards revenue generation, primarily revolving around subscriptions.

Now, with AI training and inferring on content, the conversation is doubling down on revenue generation, with focus aroudn attribution, credit and compensation.

It's not about simply having more revenue, but it's about the opportunities of new revenue models enabled by agentic payment infrastructure..

**The future of content publishing needs configurable revenue models, not just policy toggles**

Medium’s [latest update](https://medium.com/blog/writers-you-now-have-more-control-over-how-ai-companies-use-your-stories-662788e7a6ae) frames the problem well aroudn 3Cs and AI companies: **C**onsent, **C**redit, and **C**ompensation.

The current implementation is a binary toggle. Maximize reach or minimize training. Writers choose between getting cited by AI products or opting out entirely.

That is a step in the right direction. But it is not the 3Cs. It is a tradeoff between two of them.

Digg’s [recent reset](https://digg.com/) looks like a different story, but it is not. **If bots, spam, and synthetic engagement dominate the system, trust disappears and the product breaks.**

Slop and noise does not lead to high quality content, or revenue for content creators. Same root issue.

The internet still runs on economics that assume human attention is scarce, content is static, and abuse is manageable. None of that is true anymore.

Content is now consumed by readers, crawlers, LLMs, agents, answer engines, summarizers, scrapers, and bot networks. Engagement is cheap to fake. Distribution is increasingly intermediated. Focus is increasingly scrace. Attention is increasingly fragmented.

## Not Everyone Wants the Same Thing

We started working on [Grove.city](https://grove.city/) late last year to make sure that content creators can engage their audience and earn what they deserve for high-quality content. It doesn't matter if the creator or consumer is a human or agent, high quality earns what it deserves.

Speaking with various content creators, we learnt that not everyone wants the same thing. Some are looking for a way to engage their audience. Some want to make a living or build a business around it. Some are building a personal brand. Others just do it as a hobby.

All of this results in tradeoffs that compete with each other: moentization, exclusivity, exposure, reach, distribution, quality, engagement, attention, and more.

But the current landscape forces everyone into the same set of tradeoffs. If you want reach, you give up revenue. If you want revenue, you gate content and lose reach. If you want engagement, you optimize for algorithms that reward volume over quality.

Content platforms and their creators have a few choices: banners, ads, sponsors, subscriptions, or hoping for a miracle through attribution after value has already been extracted.

They need a new primitive: **a way to attach lightweight pricing and permissions to access, interaction, and machine consumption.**

Not a harder paywall. Not another subscription funnel. Not _“block AI”_ as a slogan.

It'll also be much easier to distill which headlines are being shared and which posts are eing read.

Most platforms live in one corner. High reach and low revenue. Or high revenue and low reach. The subscription middle ground works for some content creators, but it requires a loyal audience before it generates meaningful income.

## What Options Do We Have?

This isn't an exhaustive list, but just a few examples companies like medium could implement to continue enabling reach, create revenue opportunities, and account for AI access.

### 1. Pay for preview

A reader gets the first layer for free, then pays a small amount to unlock the rest.

Not a monthly commitment. Not a registration maze. Just a lightweight way to monetize high-intent access.

### 2. Pay to unlock

One tap to unlock an article, dataset, archive item, comment thread, download, or source material.

This creates a middle ground the web mostly skipped over: not free, not subscription, just paid access when value is actually requested.

### 3. Pay to comment

If posting is free, spam scales.

If commenting requires a small payment or stake, low-effort abuse becomes more expensive. Quality goes up. Moderation gets easier. Community trust improves.

### 4. Paid machine access

Agents, crawlers, and AI products should not have only two modes: scrape for free or get blocked.

There should be a third option: pay for access under explicit terms.

That is the piece the web is still missing.

### 5. Leave a tip

We've all heard of Sentiment Analaysis, but how about Sentiment Creation.

Imagine frontier labs had a _tipping-jar_ they would give to agents that can leave a tip during training or inference runs if they deemed your content useful?

## What the 3Cs Look Like With Real Infrastructure

Medium named the right goals. Here is what each one looks like when you build the economic layer underneath:

- **Consent** — permissions per action, not per platform. Preview for free, full text for a micropayment, training access at a different price.
- **Credit** — baked into the transaction. If an agent pays to access content, the citation is part of the receipt. Not dependent on goodwill.
- **Compensation** — real-time and direct. Writers earn when content is accessed, not when a platform negotiates a bulk licensing deal later.

That is the kind of infrastructure we have been building at [Grove](https://grove.city).

## This is Not Just About Monetization

This is also about trust.

Spam is a business model because the marginal cost of abuse is close to zero.

AI slop spreads for the same reason.

Most platforms have tried to solve this with policy, moderation, classifiers, or identity layers alone. Those matter, but they are incomplete. When the economic cost of low-quality behavior is zero, abuse keeps finding a way back in.

Small amounts of friction change that.

Not enough friction to break the product. Just enough to make intent legible.

That matters for comments. It matters for previews. It matters for premium content. And it increasingly matters for machine access.

Publishers should be able to set terms like:

- this can be previewed, but full access costs money
- this can be cited, but not trained on
- this can be queried by agents, but only through a payment path
- this thread is open to read, but posting requires stake
- this archive is available, but metered

Those are product decisions. They should also be economic decisions.

The point is not to force every interaction into a transaction.

The point is to give publishers more control over where free stops and value begins.

That matters more now because AI is changing the shape of traffic itself. More discovery will happen through interfaces that summarize, extract, and route around the destination. More traffic will come from software, not just people. More “users” will be agents acting on behalf of users. If publishers cannot price and permission that world, they will keep losing leverage inside it.

## The Direction is Already Clear

Cloudflare is building [pay-per-crawl](https://developers.cloudflare.com/ai-crawl-control/features/pay-per-crawl/what-is-pay-per-crawl/) into the CDN layer. The frontier labs are not far behind. As AI products become the primary interface for consuming content, it is only a matter of time before they start tipping or compensating the sources they rely on. The incentive is there. The models are only as good as the content they train on and retrieve from. If that content dries up or gets gated, the products get worse.

**The future of publishing needs pricing power at the level of individual actions.**

Not just subscriptions.

Not just ads.

Not just hoping platforms do the right thing.

## What This Could Look Like for Medium

Right now, Medium asks AI companies to respect opt-out signals. Some do. Some do not. Writers get a toggle.

With the right infrastructure, a platform like Medium could instead:

- let writers set per-story pricing for AI training access
- charge agents and crawlers for full-text retrieval, with compensation going directly to the author
- gate commenting with small stakes to reduce spam without reducing participation
- offer paid previews as a lightweight alternative to hard paywalls

That is the direction we have been building toward at [Grove](https://grove.city).

The best place to start is not a full business model overhaul.

It is one controlled surface:

- paid previews
- stake-gated commenting
- agent-paid access
- metered unlocks for premium content

Small surface area. Clear incentives. Measurable outcomes.

The web is not going back.

But it can get better economic primitives.

If you run a publishing platform, media property, or community product and are thinking about this, we should talk.
