## Make plans without expectations

Since Andrew's blog first came out, I replied to over half a dozen of his emails.

Even though my messages were sometimes witty, fun, or insightful, busy people don't have time to get distracted by everyone's two cents, even when those cents are made of gold.

## Should I buy a company?

We met at [Hey Happy](https://www.heyhappycoffee.com/) and got straight into it.

Since I know just about everything Andrew has shared publicly through his book, blog, and podcasts, I got to share a bit about myself.

We talked about Vyvanse being an open secret for happiness among people with ADHD, along with other parts of our supplement stacks. At one point, he pulled about a dozen pills out of his pocket and set them on the table. As someone who has been looking into protein powder, creatine, and BCAAs since 2005, I thought: this is my guy.

If you're an entrepreneur, you're likely a workaholic, which often comes with an addictive personality. That tends to spill over into other addictions, both good and bad. I wrote more about this topic [here](./../thoughts/2025-12-23-addiction-adhd-discipline-and-obsession.md).

By the end of the conversation, Andrew could tell that I have ambitions to build impactful, deep-tech companies. He could also tell that I don't yet have the financial freedom to do that as effectively as I want.

That's when he planted the seed: why not buy an existing company, clean up the operations, cut the burn, and milk the cigar butt for everything that's left?

At first, I questioned how a revenue-generating company could need that much help.

Companies require founders with vision, grit, persistence, tolerance for pain, and adaptability. They require timing, real value creation, a true zero-to-one, and a sprinkle of luck.

Then it clicked. There are plenty of founders who had a good run in a particular niche at a particular time and would happily let go if the price, and a few other things, are right. There are also many people from older generations who built small businesses over decades and don't know what to do with them next.

For software companies, this is easier than ever today:

1. Review expenses and cut what you don't need
2. Move cloud workloads to bare metal
3. Automate with AI workflows
4. Scale the team down to the minimum

If you start with a product that generates revenue and provides value, the playbook is simple.

This is essentially what private equity does, but there's a real opportunity for a consulting shop that focuses on this without any ambition to scale the business itself.

## Showing Up, Building Relationships, Enjoying Every Moment and Making Things Happen

This trip worked out as well as it possibly could have, even though I had no real plan and accepted that my original goal might not happen.

It gave me space to reflect and be grateful. I had a rough plan, one simple goal, and no expectations.

One weakness I've adapted around over the past few years is learning to be more flexible, more adaptive, and better at letting things go.

I wasn't planning to explore Victoria, visit Butchart Gardens, go to a comedy show, or have anyone join me on this mini-adventure. I was planning to meet Andrew.

Up until the morning I was supposed to leave, expectations didn't match reality, but I enjoyed every moment anyway.

I kept asking, texting, and nudging things forward. Instead of a group lunch, I ended up with a one-on-one hour with Andrew. I got to know him beyond his public persona, which isn't all that different, and he got to know me.

I met a ton of interesting people along the way and couldn't have asked for more.

If you're one of the people I met on this trip and somehow made it this far, thank you.

## Reflecting on something I should've said better

You know that moment when you say something in public and only later realize it could have landed cleaner?

Yeah. That happened at ELC.

One of the prompts was: **‚ÄúWhat is something you want to do less of?‚Äù**

My answer: **‚ÄúSpend less time talking to investors.‚Äù**

A bold choice, given I was sitting in a room full of current and future investors.

What I should have said, more clearly, is this.

There are a few fundamentally different ways people approach capital:

1. Always be raising. Money is momentum. Take it when it‚Äôs offered.
2. Build something durable first, then raise to accelerate.
3. Optimize for financial independence and optionality, not scale at all costs.

All three paths require relationship-building. When those relationships are aligned and genuine, they don‚Äôt feel like work.

My friction hasn‚Äôt been with investors as a category. It‚Äôs been with incentives.

Spending years in crypto exposed me to too many conversations centered on quick flips, pump-and-dumps, and zero-sum games that reshuffle value instead of creating it. I‚Äôve had people want to invest without understanding the product. I‚Äôve had others ask me to build things explicitly for short-term speculation.

That‚Äôs not what I‚Äôm interested in.

I move fast. I care deeply about execution and revenue. But I believe investor returns should come _after_ real value creation, not before.

If that closes some doors, they weren‚Äôt doors I wanted open.
