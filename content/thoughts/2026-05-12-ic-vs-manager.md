If you're a seasoned software engineer, you've probably come across the question: _"Should you be a manager or an IC?"_

A lot of ink has been spilled over that question, and my own opinions have also evolved.

As I started interviewing and thinking about my next career move, I found myself deeply reflecting on that question.

More importantly, the approach to answering that question today is very different than it was 5 or 10 years ago.

But first, some background. I still have a lot to learn and experience, so I'll share what I have and haven't done.

## Some Background

**What I have done**:

- **12+ years** as a professional software engineer (i.e. an individual contributor)
- **3+ years** of that formally managing a team of 5-10 engineers at a startup of 50 people at its peak
- Operated as both a Tech Lead and Senior Engineer at companies ranging in size from 500 - 3,000 people
- Co-founded and shut down three companies

**What I haven't done**:

- Worked as a Principal Individual Contributor at very large (>10k employees) companies
- Managed a team larger than 10 people
- Held an executive role at large companies
- Started a successful company

The experience is broad, but limited, and I'm excited to keep compounding on it.

As I've been thinking about my next career move, I reflected on whether I want to be an individual contributor, a manager, both, or something in between. One of the great things about the AI transformation is that line is much blurrier than it used to be.

You don't have to commit to just one at any point in time, but you do have to focus on just one at a time. In other words, executives can still contribute as ICs, but it cannot be their primary responsibility. Similarly, ICs have more room to contribute to leadership decisions, but it cannot be their primary responsibility.

## Staff Archetypes

Will Larson outlines 4 staff archetypes in his book:

- **Tech Lead**: Pairs with manager(s) to execute on one or more priorities. This often leaks into Tech Lead Manager (TLM) role with lightweight people management.
- **Architect**: Sets the direction, quality, standards and approach in a specific domain. This often requires very deep technical knowledge under critical constraints.
- **Solver**: A problem solver that keeps a close line of communication with leadership to jump into and in between ambiguous areas, with lots of uncertainty, and provides a lot of context that is used to shape strategy and align on priorities.
- **Right Hand**: Borrows scope and authority from executive to extent their reach and attention in larger organizations to handle a wide range of issues that would otherwise be escalated to an executive.

## Takeaway

Having spent 12+ years as a software engineer, 3+ of which were spent formally managing 5-10 individuals, I've developed a nuanced perspective.

- Managers and ICs lead
- They all play a key role in organizations
- They all infelunce tech, financial and strategic decisisons
- They all do 1:1s
- They all write reviews
- They're all player coaches
- One is cogniciant of finances, the other thinks about finances and improves them
- One is cognician of technical tradeoffs, the other thinks about technical tradeoffs and improves them
- Both do: 4 hours of meetings, planning, reviews, recruiting, etc
- Both do: coding, developing, etc
- Both participate in design
- One spends 4 hours hours building, and code quality
- One spends 4 hours on priorirty and strategy
- Both need to scale themselves
