Bitcoin’s First Mover Advantage

One way of looking at blockchain is simply as some protocol used to design the world’s most tamper proof database. With the exception of few true altruists in the world, everyone likes money and has a self-interest in getting more of it. With money being so important to us, why not use the world’s most secure database to keep track of how much money everyone has?

I personally believe that blockchain will have many different applications in the future, with prediction markets being a use case I’m particularly excited about. However, store of value is the most obvious and popular use case that’ll most likely come to fruition first. In this post I discuss why Bitcoin’s first mover advantage and how it’s recent price action compares to that in the past.

A social network only is only valuable if it has users. The infrastructure is built by the developers of the social network, and it becomes valuable once it produces revenue. Bitcoin is completely useless unless it has all three, so things are a little backwards and it’s one of the reasons why things feel more speculative than they are.

First Mover Advantage

Depending on the situation, being the first, second or late mover has its advantages and disadvantages. Facebook was an early, but far from first mover after Friendster, Myspace and a few others in the social networking space. Microsoft was also a later mover in the mobile operating system space after Google and Apple, but that turned out completely differently. Discussing the reasons for why things turned out the way they did could be a book of it’s own, but it’s interesting to note where in the technology stack each of these services lie.

There is always friction to move users from one service or platform to another. I’m likely to give a new service a try if it involves going to a different website, less likely if I can’t use my preferred web browser, much less likely to try it if I need to use a different operating system, and extremely unlikely if I need different hardware. The level of friction increases as you go down the stack, and is further magnified if the service is not one that has mainstream adoption.

Networking protocols are engrained even deeper into our technology stack. The TCP/IP protocol, which still still at the core of how the web works, was adopted by ARAPANET in 1983. The most major change to this this pair of protocols in the past 35 years was the introduction of IPv6, which was originally published in 1995 and is currently adopted by 21% of Google users. Similarly, the RFC for HTTP 1.1 was published in 1997, and only followed by HTTP 2.0 in 2015, which is currently adopted by 23% of the web. These protocols are not necessarily the best solution to the problem, but they work and evolve slowly when truly detrimental problems arise — such as hitting the maximum number of IP addresses.

A protocol used by a couple people is just an experiment. A protocol with mass adoption is almost immobile. This is why the language we speak, the calendar system we use, and the monetary system we’ve adopted work. Spoken languages develop very slowly over time, and new languages such as Esperanto have a really hard time being adopted by the mainstream.

I believe we’re going to see many different blockchains used for many different things in the future, but Bitcoin’s first mover advantage as a store of value protocol gives it an enormous first mover advantage. There are data centers (mining farms), built out with hardware dedicated to solely mining bitcoin. Bitcoin currently has a hashrate of 12.5 TH/s.
