Simulation of Cyclic Adenosine Monophosphate on Glucose Stability

tl;dr Enable simulating the effects of Cyclic Adenosine Monophosphate (cAMP) modulation through software to understand the breadth and depth of its effect on biophysical and biochemical processes as a path to solving various health problems and improving general health.

Motivation: Efim Liberman pioneered the field of molecular computing by theorizing that quantum computing is utilized in finding solutions to complex intracellular open-loop systems. His work led to the formation of a new science, Chaimatics, that aims to unite biology, physics and mathematics on 4 basic principles. In practice, his work was driven by studying the electrical nerve cell reaction to cAMP and observing its effects on membrane permeability. The importance of cAMP has been studied and ranges from autoimmune disease, inflammation, obesity, depression and more. A regular stream of literature has been published from the 1950s to the present day, but the simulation infrastructure is lacking.

Goal: The goal of this proposal is to create a simulation stack that enables others to easily explore, understand and simulate the role and modulation of cAMP under different conditions.

Introduction: cAMP was first discovered by Dr. Earl Sutherland in 1958 for which he received a Nobel prize. It plays a crucial role as a secondary messenger in many biological processes such as:

  • Signal transduction: Intra-cellular communication of message transmission such as Hormones

  • PKA (Protein Kinase A) Activation: cAMP binding to enzymes to regulate gene expression

With cAMP being established as a universal regulator of metabolism and gene expression, it is continually being studied as a mechanism to solve neurological or mood disorders. Some finite element analyses (FEA) have been built to determine its effects in pulmonary vasculature but were only created as a tool to supplement experimental studies on intracellular cAMP signalling.

Approach & Methodology: Though the scope of cAMP’s impact is very large, there is still no shared framework or standard on how cAMP effects can be simulated. However, by using open-source tools such as BioNetGen or PySB, and initially targeting a specific simulation, we can easily enable further research. Given the prevalence of health science and the increased focus on diabetes prevention in recent past, the focus of this project will be to build and open-source a software stack that is able to simulate the effects of cAMP signalling to drive glucose homeostasis (i.e. stability) and lipolysis (i.e. fat breakdown) as previously explored in some work.

Challenges & Risks: One of the reasons this software simulation stack hasn’t been readily available is due to the lack of overlap in the modern software engineering industry and biochemistry/biophysics. This creates an opportunity to leverage the benefits of modern software tooling (configurability, observability, UX, AI advancements, etc.…) to reduce the feedback loop of future experimentation. However, it also creates a risk in whether this simulation will be useful at all as a precursor to validation in physical organisms. cAMP simulation could be done by modulating its concentration, by introducing exogenous chemical elements (e.g. potassium, sodium, magnesium) that impact the cell membrane, or through external electric stimulation, but having had no formal professional experience in the field, I am likely to hit unknown unknowns along the way that may prevent ultimately beneficial results.
