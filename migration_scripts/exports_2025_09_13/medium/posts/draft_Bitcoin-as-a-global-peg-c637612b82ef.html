Whom do we trust?

Bitcoin comes with it’s own list of problems

  • We need to trust the bitcoin developers
  • We need to trust the developers
  • We need to trust that miners won’t collude
  • One of the benefits of open source (aside from being able to contribute yourself), is being able to use and modify the code to fit your needs. Since the source code on it’s own is useless, and security only comes from sufficient mining power and decentralization, one of the biggest upsides Richard Stallman advocates for is that you can experiment and change the software however you want. However, while a lot of software can be used on it’s own after some modifications are made, a protocol cannot. A BIP needs be posted, and even if it is fully tested and functional, it won’t necessary work unless the miners accept it.
  • The Bitcoin forks are scrutinized quite a bit, but they’re actually just open source freedom at it’s root
  • Should we talk about SegWit2X bugs? https://bitcointechtalk.com/segwit2x-bugs-explained-8e0c286124bc
  • Bitcoin as a “product” is heavily dependent on adoption before all of it’s benefits come to fruition. From a technical perspective, there needs to be a sufficiently large and decentralized set of miners in order to keep the blockchain secure, otherwise someone with enough money and incentive can come in and start double spending coins.

I wrote the response below in a bit of a rush so I apologize if it’s unclear and all of my grammatical errors. Let me know if I need to clarify anything.

============
 
 That is quite the question :). I can probably write a whole thesis surrounding that topic, but I’ll try to summarize my thoughts.

I believe that crypto currencies (digital cash) will replace how money is currently being handled and transmitted in our economy. I think it’s extremely likely that every country will have their own blockchain supporting their own currency. The interest (miner’s reward) of being a miner of this blockchain will attract enough members of that country to mine. In a simple sense, in addition to issuing bonds, individuals or companies can also become miners.

I also believe that the IMF will play a bigger role in the coming decades in terms of issuing a “world currency” in order to prevent debt creation from getting out of hand. After the Bretton woods agreement fell apart, the whole global economy has been booming, but I don’t think it’s sustainable. A global peg of some sort will be necessary, and I think it’s unlikely to be gold. Jim Rickards does a much better job than me discussing the need for “world money” in “The Road To Ruin”.

Bitcoin’s technological inferiority is very important to keep in mind. Unlike centralized servers, which can be updated after they’re launched, you probably very well know how difficult it is to achieve consensus to update a sufficiently decentralized blockchain. As a developer, I can always make bugs or improvements to services that I’ve built This is not possible with Bitcoin though.

Bitcoin’s current advantages are:

  • Large market cap: a lot of people have a financial interest in keeping Bitcoin as the primary form of cryptocurrency
  • Marketing / advertising: No matter how good or bad the tech is, marketing is important and Bitcoin has become a household name
  • Miners: It is still arguable one of the safest blockchains out there. There are thousands (I’m guessing) of mining farms out there with millions of dollars spent to mine that specific coin. All of that infrastructure can be used for mining something else with the same hash algorithm, but probably not most of the other blockchains out there.
  • Trully decentralized: Unlike ethereum (which I very much believe in), it really has no group of people that are leading the development effort. This is good because it’s difficult to modify, but bad because it’s falling behind technologically.

Bitcoin’s disadvantages are:

  • Slow
  • Expensive
  • Hard to update
  • No finality or liveness
  • Can’t scale and doesn’t have an “on chain” path to scaling. The lightning network can arguably resolve this issue.

If you consider networking protocols, like http, tcp or IP, these aren’t great protocols either. They were first, everything was built on top of them, and upgrades take years to get approved and propagate. Similarly for Bitcoin to go away, it’ll take a very long time until the mining infrastructure for a different currency is also sufficiently secure. Ethereum, for example, has A LOT of applications being built on top of it. Even if something better than ethereum comes out, all the companies will need to spend a lot of time migrating their applications. For this reason, it probably has a higher chance of not being replaced; along with it’s strong core development team.

The following point will sound very “conspiracy like”, so take it with a grain of salt. Have you read “The Fourth Turning” by Neil Howe and William Strauss? It surrounds Strauss–Howe generational theory and came to fame when they accurately predicted a crisis in 2005 (+- a couple years) even though it was written in the 90s. One of the big points they discuss is that every 80 years there’s a cycle that results in a big crisis. Approximately 15 years after this cycle, the crisis comes to a climax. For example, the 1929 market crash resulted in WWII ending in 1945. The financial crisis happened 10 years ago, and the current political state of the world is very iffy. Trump is president of the United States. Russia and China are both essentially being led under a dictatorship. There has been a ton of unbrescendated debt creation. The talks of nuclear war have started coming up again. Similar to the spark that ignited the first world war, I think a big change will come in the next decade, but I don’t know what it is. I don’t think there will be a global war, but I believe that it might result in there being a global monetary peg. If this is the case, Bitcoin is a likely candidate to be that peg for many reasons (censorship free, decentralized, fixed supply, secured by cryptography, etc…) .

I won’t argue that Bitcoin is not a volatile Fad, but it’s networking effects are remarkable. If you look at Metcalfe’s Law, it’s continuously gaining a lot of power simply due to how well it’s known. I don’t know how likely a large international monetary movement is, but Bitcoin is likely to be at the core of it provided it happens. When it comes to having a global peg and a world reserve currency, a 10 minute transaction time is not any country’s worry.
